Plasmodium vivax: Classification and Structure

Classification

Plasmodium vivax is a protozoan parasite responsible for the most widespread form of recurring malaria in humans. It belongs to the domain Eukaryota, as it possesses a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Within the kingdom Protista (Protozoa), it falls under the phylum Apicomplexa, characterized by the presence of a unique apical complex used to penetrate host cells. It belongs to the class Aconoidasida, order Haemosporida, and family Plasmodiidae. Its genus is Plasmodium, and its species is vivax.

Structure

The structure of Plasmodium vivax varies significantly throughout its complex two-host life cycle. In the infectious stage, known as the sporozoite, the parasite is elongated, spindle-shaped, and equipped with an apical complex containing micronemes and rhoptries for invading liver cells.

Inside human red blood cells, it transforms into the trophozoite, initially appearing as a delicate signet ring with a peripheral nucleus and a central vacuole. It later matures into a larger amoeboid form that feeds on hemoglobin and produces yellowish-brown hemozoin pigment granules.

During asexual reproduction, it develops into a schizont, a multinucleated structure containing numerous merozoites ready to infect new red blood cells.

Finally, some merozoites differentiate into gametocytes, which are large, oval or round sexual forms with a compact nucleus. These are ingested by the female Anopheles mosquito, where the parasite completes its life cycle.
